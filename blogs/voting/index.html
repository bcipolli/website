Prop 3 is interesting to me, for one major reason:

According to the "NO" side,

This gigantic spending initiative is framed as helping "children's hospitals," using "children" as the justification for circumventing the normal legislative process by which state spending priorities are better determined. Yet a careful reading of the definition of "children's hospital" reveals that 80% of the money may go to any acute hospital so long as it treats children, among other patients. It appears that a driving force behind this measure is to provide a backdoor way of compensating hospitals for treating indigents (including illegal aliens) who don't pay their way through the front door. (emphasis mine)
I am a strong believer that our current policy on illegal aliens is just about right. The latest Bush stuff, erecting fences here and there, that's silliness. I think we depend on having a certain number of illegal aliens in our country to do work that just about no american will do--seasonal, migratory work.

These people, and these systems, depend on having some amount of health care. If that's funded by a back-asswards bill labeled "Children's Hospitals", then it fits right in with the rest of the "don't ask, don't tell" system we have in place.

Vote YES on Prop 3.

Prop 4: Waiting Period and Parentla Notification ...

Prop 4 boils down to this for me:

We have defined "minor" and "parent/legal guardian" roles because we believe children, those under the age of 18, are not full PEOPLE yet, that they are not ready to make decisions on their own.

Now ask yourselves this: are the vast majority of girls under 18 ready to make the right decision as to whether or not it would be to their benefit to include their parents/legal guardian in a discussion about abortion? That's a big decision, one that should be about more than just emotion and fear.

I believe some young adults are poised to make such a decision, but I don't think a vast majority are. I believe it is right to define a difference between an adult and a minor. I'm not sure 18 is the "right" date, but that's not what this prop is about.

I think this prop is close enough to the right ideal that it's worth trying. Vote YES on Prop 4.

Prop 5: Nonviolent Drug Offenses

Here are the major high-level points that I think must be addressed:

1. Although new spending is proposed, given the figures in the proposition, the state will come out ahead due to decreases expenses from prisons.

2. For all the "bad" possible side-effects of Prop 5, are they unique to Prop 5, or would they be true in some form or another for any large-scale rehabilitation program? I think it is the latter, not the former.

3. In that case, do we ever want to give large-scale rehabilitation a try? If so, and if you're with me on (2), then I think it's time to vote for Prop 5.

It never seems like a good time for a large change. Things are either too hard or too good. You're either possibly disrupting during a time of destabilization, or disrupting during a time of wealth.

It never seems like a good time for risk and unintended consequences. But in that case, we'll never do anything good.

Vote YES on Prop 5!

Prop 6: Police and Law Enforcement Funding

Prop 6 is a great example of how our voting system is broken. Prop 6 is incomprehensible. The words are incomprehensible. The scope is incomprehensible. The effect over the state--what citizen could possibly know? It's absolutely ridiculous to ask us to vote on this. Our vote will never be based in real understanding.

With that said, I wanted to see how different people were lining up with regards to Prop 6. I saw that the District Attorney of San Diego FOR Prop 6, as is the sheriff of LA County. I think San Diego and Los Angeles are two important regions where help is needed. I'm not convinced that the opponents of Prop 6 live in urban areas of need.

I am not a fan of throwing money at a problem. I wish for rehabilitation over punishment. I think we use our jails in silly ways. And I think voting YES on Prop 6 is our best option. To turn your back on a problem doesn't make it go away. Let's try this.

Prop 7: Renewable Energy Generation

I want to vote YES on Prop 7. I don't care that it's too aggressive, puts a financial burden on us, that it asks for too much. I don't care that it shuts out smaller producers. But...

When time and time again you read scathing articles on Prop 7, like this one, when you read that the length of contracts would shut down the competitive pricing process, when you read that the bill is "rushed", "poorly written", over and over...
