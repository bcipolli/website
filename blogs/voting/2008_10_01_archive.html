Prop 6: Police and Law Enforcement Funding

Prop 6 is a great example of how our voting system is broken. Prop 6 is incomprehensible. The words are incomprehensible. The scope is incomprehensible. The effect over the state--what citizen could possibly know? It's absolutely ridiculous to ask us to vote on this. Our vote will never be based in real understanding.

With that said, I wanted to see how different people were lining up with regards to Prop 6. I saw that the District Attorney of San Diego FOR Prop 6, as is the sheriff of LA County. I think San Diego and Los Angeles are two important regions where help is needed. I'm not convinced that the opponents of Prop 6 live in urban areas of need.

I am not a fan of throwing money at a problem. I wish for rehabilitation over punishment. I think we use our jails in silly ways. And I think voting YES on Prop 6 is our best option. To turn your back on a problem doesn't make it go away. Let's try this.

Prop 9: Criminal Justice & Victim's Rights

oy, this one is hard.

On the one hand, there are some big red flags on this measure:
* proposed by a single entity, no doubt out of emotion of loss
* will cost us money, trouble, and create even more paperwork.
* argument is that it will duplicate existing law

On the other hand, it seems reasonable:
* put certain rights for victims into the state constitution, as they are for criminals' rights.
* it sounds "right".

With that said, there are two things that worry me the most:
* the YES for Prop 9 argument uses single cases, but has no statistics. I don't trust such arguments.
* It takes a 3/4 vote to remove this from the state constitution. In other words, I better be really confident before I vote.

I'm not. Vote NO for Prop 9!

Prop B: Bayfront Redevelopment Initiative

Bottom line, after reading the arguments, I'm *NOT* convinced that the development will be a positive benefit for San Diego. The YES people sound too much like people grabbing for money; the NO people sound more reasonable.

Prop 11: Redistricting

Prop 11 is complex and confusing. Right now, after census is taken every 10 years, some set of people redraw district lines "accordingly." According to proponents of Prop 11, existing elected officials use this opportunity to redraw district lines to try and guarantee their own re-election--a strong conflict of interest.

Prop 11 is the fruit of this concern. It creates a new commission, some bureaucracy for executing the redistricting, and a whole lotta headaches.

To me, Prop 11:
  • Costs more
  • Is more confusing
  • Doesn't seem to guarantee any improvment over existing methods. The non-elected commission, who says they'll do a better job?
Is it worth all the headache? I don't see that proponents have made their arguments a no-brainer and, because it sounds like it costs more AND makes things more complex, I vote NO on Prop 11.

Prop 12: Veteran's Bond Act of 2008

Prop 12 is about providing low-cost loans to US veterans. If they're unable to make payments sufficient to pay back their loans, the state will subsidize them.

Nobody disputes the need of such a plan. Opponents of Prop 12, however, want to offer the plan to some veterans and not others. They want to draw the line at "veterans who served in the time of a war". Apparently they are the "most deserving" veterans and, under stressful times, we should limit things.

I strongly disagree with this parceling of service. Who are we to rate service as greater or lesser? Anybody serving our country is making a great sacrifice. None know ahead of time what the future will hold. To separate in this case is to throw a big, thankless FUCK YOU back at those men and women who did what most of us would not do ourselves and might not even let our children do.

Don't show a lack of appreciation for these sacrifices; we've had ENOUGH instances of lacking appreciation for veterans. Vote YES on Prop 12.

Prop A: Regional Firefighting Property Tax

Prop A is kind of funny. In some sense, there's a great need for funding for firefighting--no doubt about it. Opponents simply whine that it's a bad time to be taxed; we're struggling to pay bills!

Nobody would suggest foregoing health or car insurance when in a pinch--they are necessities in our lives. To go without fire insurance in this region is just nutty. Having property owners pay the tax only makes sense--they're the ones who ultimately have the property and structures being protected.

On the other hand, I'm really against the current model of "fight small fires" and ultimately forcing unmanageable ones--by definition!--to burn. Ultimately we need controlled, frequent burns. Burns are natural, the carbon will be released either at a slow pace or at a delayed-then-fast one. Because of that, I'm reluctant to vote for a measure supporting this model for dealing with brush.

Ultimately, any plan needs money, and I don't see that the controlled burns model is an option anywhere. With that in mind, I choose to get that money. Vote YES on Prop A!

Prop C: Use of Mission Bay Excess Funds

Prop C boils down to a proposition telling our governing bodies that we don't trust them to do "the right thing". The amount of money being allocated for spending is basically not spending. However, the use of the funds is changing drastically.

Most importantly 75% of the excess funds generated by Mission Bay will be required to be spent on Mission Bay improvements. Many people take it as self-evident that money generated there should "stay" there. I guess this comes from the same line of thought that says Obama's tax plan is socialist--those who earn money should keep it.

If that was the case, we wouldn't have any taxes nor government. Individuals share resources to keep our entire country at a certain level of income and health--this winds up feeding back to the rich who may not need to worry in the short term. The health of our nation protects the health of their investments, businesses, and ability to live safely.

With that said, Prop C was put onto the ballot almost unanimously. The two dissenters were basically saying that the reduced flexibility might come back to bite us in the ass. True, but apparently we don't trust our elected officials and their appointees. We feel safer telling them what to do with the money.

I think it's more likely for officials to misuse money than it is for us to have real trouble due to reduced flexibility. We should also protect and keep up those areas that generate revenue for us. 25% of that excess revenue goes to other sources, and by using 75% of excess revenue to repair and expand Mission Bay, we're essentially protecting our valuable revenue source.

So, I vote YES on Prop D

Prop D: Alcohol Consumption on Beaches

This proposition seems entirely idealistic. It has nothing to do with budgets or anything. Interestingly, neither the Republican party NOR the Democratic party take an official position on this proposition!

So here we are. On the one hand, democracy demands choice. Leave it to people to do the right thing. Self-govern. On the other hand, democracy demands education and exercise of self-control, and neither of those things are happening.

If democracy was really about choice, we wouldn't have a drinking age, nor would we outlaw drugs or do a million of the other big-brother things that we do. So, I don't believe in the ideal. Arguing ideals is vacuous.

Then, what we argue is ... is alcoholic consumption bringing down the quality of our beaches? Would our beaches be better off without alcohol consumption? I'd say that, without a clear answer, we should allow things. Unless they're clearly problematic, we have no reason to impose laws and force ourselves to try and enforce them.

I haven't heard of any good argument on why our beaches would be better off without alcohol. I can see some small pluses (maybe safer, less rowdy crowd?), but also minuses (fewer people, less lively, less hot-spotty).

I don't see any good arguments at smartvoter.org either (just found the site; awesome!), so I vote NO.

Mission Statement

I've got my absentee ballot in front of me. On 90% of the things I can vote on, I have no information other than the 3 sentence blurb on the ballot itself. How many people like myself vote based on these blurbs? Where do the blurbs come from? Is this really democracy?

I submit that it's not democracy unless each voter is educated. The whole "vote!" movement has it backwards--the first step is to get EDUCATED on what's happening. If after being educated you're not motivated to vote, then I will wish that I'd be living your life.

Each post in this blog will find bipartisan information on a single voting issue on the ballot. I'll be rockin' and rollin' through them, posting up links, thoughts, then a reasoned final decision, until I finish each and every entry in my California ballot.
